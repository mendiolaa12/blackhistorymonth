Benjamin Banneker 1731–1806

Benjamin Banneker was a self-taught African American scientist whose work encompassed astronomy, mathematics, mechanical engineering, and surveying. His publications and practical achievements challenged eighteenth-century assumptions about race and intellect.

Astronomy & Almanacs Surveying Washington, D.C. Clockmaking Letter to Jefferson

Astronomy & Early Almanacs

Between 1792 and 1797 Banneker published a series of almanacs containing precise astronomical calculations: predictions of solar and lunar eclipses, planetary motions, tide tables, and seasonal weather guidance. These publications circulated across the Mid-Atlantic and demonstrated his command of astronomical mathematics. They provided one of the clearest early proofs of African American scientific authorship in the early Republic.

Surveying of Washington, D.C.

In 1791 Banneker joined Major Andrew Ellicott as scientific assistant on the federal survey to locate the new national capital. He performed nighttime celestial observations and computations—fixing latitude and longitude and establishing reference points crucial to boundary alignment. His calculations helped preserve the capital’s geometric integrity after Pierre Charles L'Enfant's departure and constitute an early documented example of African American participation in federal scientific work.

Innovative Clockmaker

Circa 1753, Banneker built a striking wooden clock after examining a pocket watch. Having never seen a clock mechanism before, he disassembled the watch mentally and hand-carved every gear and component in wood, producing a clock that reportedly ran for more than forty years. The timepiece demonstrates his practical engineering skill and applied mathematics.

Anti-Slavery Correspondence

In 1791 Banneker sent Thomas Jefferson a letter challenging the intellectual foundations of slavery and invoking natural-rights principles of the Declaration of Independence. He enclosed his astronomical calculations as demonstrative evidence. Jefferson acknowledged the letter and circulated Banneker’s work; the exchange remains one of the most prominent early public challenges to slavery authored by a free African American.
